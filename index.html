Pulsar Polar Cap Discharge

(Timokhin & Harding 2015)
  • Direct PIC simulations of this process have shown that pair production proceeds in bursts.
  • Electric field is screened by the freshly produced pairs, but as pairs outflow, the electric field is induced again.
  • The whole process approaches a limit cycle.

Goals

We want to write down a minimal kinetic model that can reproduce the limit-cycle behavior of pulsar polar cap $e^\pm$ cascade.


A theoretical model for the polar cap $e^\pm$ discharge may help us better understand pulsar radio emission (e.g. Philippov et al 2020). It may also help constrain the production of axion-like particles from the $\mathbf{E}\cdot\mathbf{B}$ oscillations.

Takuya Okawa

Pair Production Limit Cycle

A few ingredients are needed for the pair production cycle:

  • Advection of pairs from the gap
  • Induction of $E_\parallel$ due to $\nabla\times\mathbf{B}$
  • Pair production that responds to $E_\parallel$

Model Results

Kinetic Model

We start with the 1D Vlasov system:

$$ \frac{\partial E}{\partial t} = c(\nabla\times \mathbf{B})_{\parallel} - 4\pi j = 4\pi(j_0 - j) $$ $$ \frac{\partial f_\pm}{\partial t} + v \frac{\partial f_\pm}{\partial x} + q_{\pm}E \frac{\partial f_\pm}{\partial p} = S_{\pm} $$

Kinetic Model

For the Vlasov equation, we assume a length scale $L$ of the gap region and pairs flow out at near speed of light, replacing $\partial/\partial x$ with $1/L$ and $v$ with $c$.

$$ \frac{\partial f_\pm}{\partial t} + v \frac{\partial f_\pm}{\partial x} + q_{\pm}E \frac{\partial f_\pm}{\partial p} = S_{\pm} $$
$$ \frac{\partial f_\pm}{\partial t} + \frac{c}{L}f_\pm + q_{\pm}E \frac{\partial f_\pm}{\partial p} = S_{\pm} $$

Kinetic Model

The electric current can be directly computed from the distribution function. Its time derivative is:

$$ \begin{split} \frac{\partial j}{\partial t} &= \sum_{s=\pm} q_s\int v\frac{\partial f_s}{\partial t}\,dp \\ &= -\frac{c}{L}j + \sum_{s=\pm}\frac{q_s^2 n_s E}{m}\left<\frac{1}{\gamma_s^3}\right> - q_s\int v S_s\,dp \end{split} $$

We assume both electrons and positrons are producing secondary pairs. The integral of the source term then becomes zero.

Kinetic Model

Combining the $\partial j/\partial t$ equation and the $\partial E/\partial t$ equation, we get:

$$ \frac{\partial^{2}E}{\partial t^{2}} + \frac{c}{L}\frac{\partial E}{\partial t} + \sum_{s=\pm}\frac{4\pi n_s q_s^2}{m}\left<\frac{1}{\gamma^{3}_{s}}\right>E = \frac{4\pi c j_0}{L}. $$

This is essentially a driven and damped oscillator with variable natural frequency $\bar{\omega}^2 = \omega_p^2\langle 1/\gamma^3\rangle$.

A particular solution exists when $\bar{\omega} \approx 0$: $E\propto 4\pi j_0 t$. This solution describes the electric field induction when plasma density is low.

Kinetic Model

In order to close the equations, we need to evolve both $n_\pm$ and $\langle1/\gamma^3\rangle$:

$$ \frac{\partial n_{\pm}}{\partial t} = -\frac{c}{L}n_{\pm} + \int S_{\pm}\,dp. $$
$$ \frac{\partial\langle p_{\pm}\rangle}{\partial t} = -\frac{1}{n_{\pm}}\int S_{\pm}\langle p_{\pm}\rangle\,dp + q_{\pm}E $$

We use $1/\langle \gamma\rangle^3 \approx 1/\langle p\rangle^3$ as a crude approximation for $\langle1/\gamma^3\rangle$.

Pair Production Model

We experimented a series of pair production models to see what is needed to have a pair production limit cycle:

  • Constant pair production: $S = g$
  • Proportional to plasma density: $S = g\sum_s n_s$
  • Proportional to average momentum: $S = g\sum_s |\langle p_s\rangle|$
  • Proportional to local electric field: $S = g|E|$
  • Proportional to electric field and density: $S = g|E|\sum_{s}n_{s}$
  • Proportional to average momentum and density: $S = g\sum_{s}|\langle p_s\rangle|n_{s}$

Pair Production Model

Only the model where pair production rate is proportional to both average momentum and plasma density, $S = g\sum_{s}|\langle p_s\rangle|n_{s}$, exhibits a limit cycle.


This model is also the most physically motivated. The energy loss rate of a single particle due to curvature radiation scales as $P_\mathrm{CR} \sim \gamma^4$, while curvature photon energy $\epsilon_\mathrm{CR} \sim \gamma^3$. The number of pairs produced scales with $n\gamma$.

Long Wavelength Alfv√©n waves

  • Derive a semi-analytic solution for Alfv√©n waves propagating in dipole magnetic field

  • Understand the nonlinear wave conversion process using a perturbative expansion

  • Extend the theory to a rotating magnetosphere

Alfv√©n Wave Solution

Introduce dipole coordinates:

$$ q = \frac{\cos\theta}{r^{2}},\quad p = \frac{r}{\sin^{2}\theta},\quad \phi = \phi, $$

$q$ varies along a specific field line, and $p$ labels different field lines. $\phi$ is just the azimuthal angle. We take the axisymmetric limit where $\partial_\phi = 0$

Alfv√©n Wave Solution

Force-free equations:

$$ \begin{split} \frac{\partial \mathbf{E}}{\partial t} &= c(\nabla\times \mathbf{B}) - 4\pi\mathbf{j} \\ \frac{\partial \mathbf{B}}{\partial t} &= -c(\nabla\times \mathbf{E}) \\ \mathbf{j} &= \frac{c}{4\pi}\frac{\mathbf{B}\cdot \nabla\times \mathbf{B} - \mathbf{E}\cdot \nabla\times \mathbf{E}}{B^{2}}\mathbf{B} \\ &\quad + \frac{c}{4\pi}\nabla\cdot \mathbf{E}\frac{\mathbf{E}\times \mathbf{B}}{B^{2}}. \end{split} $$

Alfv√©n Wave Solution

Expand the electric and magnetic fields in a perturbative expansion:

$$ \begin{split} \mathbf{B} &= \mathbf{B}^{(0)} + \mathbf{B}^{(1)} + \mathbf{B}^{(2)} + \dots \\ \mathbf{E} &= \mathbf{E}^{(0)} + \mathbf{E}^{(1)} + \mathbf{E}^{(2)} + \dots \\ \end{split} $$

To first order, the force-free current is:

$$ \mathbf{j}^{(1)} = \frac{c}{4\pi}\frac{\mathbf{B}^{(0)}\cdot \nabla\times \mathbf{B}^{(1)}}{B_{0}^{2}}\mathbf{B}^{(0)}. $$

Alfv√©n Wave Solution

We seek an Alfv√©n wave solution of the following form:

$$ \mathbf{B}^{(1)} = r^{-3/2}e^{-i\omega t}f(p, q)\hat{\boldsymbol{\phi}}, $$

The goal is to determine the functional form of the unknown function $f$.

Alfv√©n Wave Solution

Equations in dipole coordinates are somewhat complicated:

$$ \begin{split} \frac{\partial\mathbf{E}^{(1)}}{\partial t} &= c\hat{\mathbf{p}}\left[-\frac{\delta}{r^{3}}\frac{\partial B_{\phi}^{(1)}}{\partial q} + \frac{3\cos\theta}{r\delta}B_{\phi}^{(1)}\right] \\ \frac{\partial\mathbf{B}^{(1)}}{\partial t} &= -c\hat{\boldsymbol{\phi}}\left[\frac{\delta}{r^{3}}\frac{\partial E_{p}^{(1)}}{\partial q} - \frac{6\cos\theta}{r\delta^{3}}(1 + \cos^{2}\theta)E_{p}^{(1)}\right], \end{split} $$

where $\delta = \sqrt{1 + 3\cos^2\theta}$.

Alfv√©n Wave Solution

Specializing to a specific field line (constant $p$), we can rewrite the equation into a very simple form:

$$ \frac{d}{d\mu}\left(\frac{1}{1 + 3\mu^{2}}\frac{df}{d\mu}\right) = -\kappa^{2}f, $$

where $\mu = \cos\theta$, and $\kappa = \omega p/c$.

Alfv√©n Wave Solution

We can solve the wave propagation on a collection of field lines:

Second-order Current

If we only have one Alfv√©n wave at 1st order, then the 2nd order force-free current is:

$$ \begin{split} \mathbf{j}^{(2)} &= \frac{c}{4\pi}\left[\frac{\mathbf{B}^{(0)}\cdot\nabla\times \mathbf{B}^{(2)}}{B_{0}^{2}}\mathbf{B}^{(0)}\right. \\ &\quad + \frac{\mathbf{B}^{(0)}\cdot\nabla\times \mathbf{B}^{(1)}}{B_{0}^{2}}\mathbf{B}^{(1)} + \left.\nabla\cdot \mathbf{E}^{(1)}\frac{\mathbf{E}^{(1)}\times \mathbf{B}^{(0)}}{B_{0}^{2}}\right]. \end{split} $$

The second line is along the $\hat{\boldsymbol{\phi}}$ direction. For a plane wave solution, $j_\phi = 0$. It is in general nonzero here due to geometric effect.

Second-order Current

Plugging in the Alfv√©n wave solution, it can be seen that there are two components of $j_\phi^{(2)}$. One with $\omega = 0$, the other with $\omega = 2\omega_A$.

Second-order Fast Waves

The $\phi$ current sources fast-magnetosonic waves at the 2nd order:

$$ \begin{split} \partial_t \mathbf{E}_{F}^{(2)} &= c(\nabla\times \mathbf{B}_{F}^{(2)}) - 4\pi \mathbf{j}_{\phi}^{(2)}, \\ \partial_t \mathbf{B}_{F}^{(2)} &= -c(\nabla \times \mathbf{B}_{F}^{(2)}). \end{split} $$

The $2\omega_A$ component sources an outgoing fms wave. The zero frequency component may source a low frequency fms wave if the primary Alfv√©n wave bounces back and forth along the flux tube (see poster by Dominic Bernardi).

Conversion Efficiency

We can in principle find the vector potential in the far zone:

$$ A_\phi^{(2)}(\mathbf{x}) = \frac{1}{c}\int j^{(2)}_{\phi}(\mathbf{x}')\frac{e^{ik|\mathbf{x} - \mathbf{x}'|}}{|\mathbf{x} - \mathbf{x}'|}\,d^{3}x' . $$

Then we can find the angular distribution and total radiation power. This gives a concrete way of computing the conversion rate of Alfv√©n waves into fast magnetosonic waves.

Conversion Efficiency

Rotating Magnetosphere

In a rotating magnetosphere, we perform a two-scale expansion in both $\delta B/B$ and $v_\phi / c$. In the closed zone, the corrections to the background magnetic field is 2nd order in rotation:

$$ \nabla\times \mathbf{B}^{(0),[2]} = 4\pi \mathbf{j}^{(0),[2]}. $$

This is because $j$ comes from $\rho_\mathrm{GJ}v_\phi$, which is a second order quantity.

Rotating Magnetosphere

We are interested in the force-free current to first order in both $\delta B/B$ and $v_\phi/c$:

$$ \begin{split} \mathbf{j}^{(1),[1]} &= \frac{c}{4\pi}\left[\frac{\mathbf{B}^{(0),[0]}\cdot \nabla\times \mathbf{B}^{(1),[1]}}{B_{0}^{2}}\mathbf{B}^{(0),[0]} \right. \\ &\quad - \nabla\cdot \mathbf{E}^{(1),[0]}\frac{\mathbf{E}^{(0),[1]}\times \mathbf{B}^{(0),[0]}}{B_{0}^{2}} \\ &\quad - \left.\nabla\cdot \mathbf{E}^{(0),[1]}\frac{\mathbf{E}^{(1),[0]}\times \mathbf{B}^{(0),[0]}}{B_{0}^{2}}\right]. \end{split} $$

Rotating Magnetosphere

Very similar to the 2nd order current in the non-rotating case, there is now $j_\phi$ at first order, which sources fast waves too:

$$ \begin{split} \partial_t \mathbf{B}_{F}^{(1),[1]} &= -c(\nabla \times \mathbf{E}_{F}^{(1),[1]}) \\ \partial_t \mathbf{E}_{F}^{(1),[1]} &= c(\nabla \times \mathbf{B}_{F}^{(1),[1]}) - 4\pi \mathbf{j}_{\phi}^{(1),[1]}. \end{split} $$

The source term $j_\phi^{(1),[1]}$ scales as $O(\delta B/B_{0})O(v_{\phi}/c)$. When $\delta B/B \ll v_\phi / c$, fast waves sourced this way has higher amplitude. Otherwise, the non-rotating 2nd order effect will win.
